# Why 5g of Creatine Isn't Enough for Your Brain: The Case for 10g

If you've been taking creatine, there's a good chance you've been taking 5 grams a day. That's the standard recommendation. It's on the label. It's what most coaches say. It's what most people in the gym do. And for your muscles, it works.

But here's what's becoming increasingly clear in the research: **your brain is a different story.**

The science behind creatine and cognition has quietly exploded over the past few years, and the consensus emerging from some of the most credible voices in longevity, neuroscience, and performance research — including Dr. Rhonda Patrick, Andrew Huberman, and Dr. Darren Candow — is pointing in the same direction. Five grams saturates your muscle. To meaningfully raise creatine levels in the brain, you likely need to go higher. And the difference matters more than most people realize.

## What Creatine Actually Does in the Brain

Most people think of creatine as a muscle supplement. But the brain is, by any measure, the most energy-hungry organ in your body — accounting for 20% of your daily energy needs despite representing only about 2% of your body weight. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/IM8-Ultimate-Essentials-Multivitamin-Supplement/dp/B0DPY59RDZ)

The brain runs on ATP — adenosine triphosphate — the same energy currency that powers your muscles during high-intensity exercise. And creatine, specifically in its phosphocreatine form, is the fastest system your body has for regenerating ATP on demand. In the brain, creatine may improve cognitive performance by increasing cellular energy reserves and reducing oxidative stress, which is particularly important in tasks requiring high cognitive processing. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/product-category/services/)

The mechanisms underlying creatine's enhancement of memory likely involve several biological pathways: increasing the energy supply to brain cells in the form of phosphocreatine, enhancing neurotransmitter synthesis including acetylcholine, and acting as a neuromodulator that affects synaptic efficacy and plasticity — both vital for learning and memory processes. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/product-category/services/)

In plain terms: creatine isn't just gym fuel. It's brain fuel. The question is whether you're taking enough for it to actually reach there.

## The Blood-Brain Barrier Problem: Why 5g Falls Short

Here's the core issue — and it's a physiological one. Creatine does not diffuse passively through cell membranes. Its uptake into the central nervous system is assured and limited by the creatine transporter operating near saturation and expressed in the endothelial cell layer of the blood-brain barrier — but not in the layer of astrocyte feet. CNS uptake of exogenous creatine from the periphery is marginal and takes a long time. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/reviews-comparisons/)

In simpler terms: your muscles are creatine sponges. They absorb it rapidly and greedily. Your brain, protected by the blood-brain barrier, is far more selective. Since creatine does not cross the blood-brain barrier readily, it is recommended that individuals consume 10g per day to promote cognitive benefit. [ThriveTools](https://thrivetools.io/)

When you're sleep-deprived, jet-lagged, or under cognitive stress, creatine seems to come to the rescue — but the brain says "we'll make our own creatine, thanks," until you're metabolically stressed. That's when the brain starts begging for more. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/IM8-Ultimate-Essentials-Multivitamin-Supplement/dp/B0DPY59RDZ)

The practical implication: at 5g per day, your muscles get what they need and your brain receives the remainder — which, after muscular uptake, may be very little. A daily dose of 10 grams of creatine doubles brain creatine levels compared to lower doses of 2 or 4 grams. Neuroimaging studies show that this higher dose increases high-energy phosphates in the gray matter, white matter, and thalamus — areas critical for mood, memory, and cognitive performance. [IM8 Health](https://im8health.com/products/essentials-pro)

## What the Research Actually Shows

The expert recommendations aren't anecdotal — they're grounded in a growing body of peer-reviewed evidence.

**The 2024 meta-analysis:** A systematic review of 16 RCTs involving 492 participants aged 20.8–76.4 years found that creatine supplementation showed significant positive effects on memory and attention time, as well as significantly improving processing speed — with subgroup analyses revealing greater benefit in individuals with diseases, those aged 18–60, and females. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/)

**The Alzheimer's pilot trial (2025):** Researchers at the University of Kansas Medical Center administered 20g of creatine per day (split into two 10g doses) to patients with Alzheimer's disease for 8 weeks. The study showed an 11% increase in brain creatine levels — and researchers saw a moderate improvement in working memory and executive function. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/personalized-guidance/) The lead researcher called the brain creatine increase "a significant finding." Read the full study at [kumc.edu](https://www.kumc.edu/about/news/news-archive/creatine-alzheimers-research.html).

**The sleep deprivation studies:** During acute oxygen deprivation, creatine supplementation at 20g per day for 7 days in young healthy individuals attenuated cognitive errors during a continuous performance test compared with placebo. In addition, following a 90-minute mentally challenging task, a high dose of creatine enhanced executive function measured by a prolonged Stroop test. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/product-tag/neuro/) This maps directly to what Patrick recommends: load up on high-demand days.

**The dose-response study:** Creatine supplementation overall increases brain creatine levels by 3–10%, but there appears to be high variability in responsiveness. Amongst studies involving young, healthy adults, three studies found improvements in brain creatine levels due to approximately 20g per day for 7–28 days, while two studies found no effect — suggesting that higher and longer dosing strategies may be needed, particularly in younger adults who may already have higher baseline brain creatine levels. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/shop/) Full study available via [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10526554/).

**The psychiatric research (2025):** A scoping review spanning 2006 to 2025 found that creatine supplementation across doses of 3–10g per day showed statistically significant improvements in depression severity, and noted that given prior evidence from studies involving sleep and oxygen deprivation using doses around 20g per day, many reviewed psychiatric studies may have used suboptimal creatine dosing — potentially underestimating its therapeutic efficacy. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/product-category/organic-foods/)

## The "Spillover" Theory: Why Muscle Saturation Is the Key

According to Dr. Darren Candow, once muscle creatine stores are saturated — which happens at around 5 grams per day — extra creatine may "spill over" to other tissues, including the brain, bones, and immune system, where it can deliver additional health and cognitive benefits. [IM8 Health](https://im8health.com/products/essentials-pro)

This is the cleanest explanation for why the 10g threshold matters: it's not that 5g does nothing for your brain. It's that at 5g, your muscles consume almost all of it before the brain gets a meaningful share. Push past muscle saturation, and the surplus has somewhere new to go.

The bottom line: 5 grams might be the baseline — but 10 grams could unlock the full-body potential of creatine. [IM8 Health](https://im8health.com/products/essentials-pro)

## A Practical Dosing Framework

Based on the research and expert recommendations, here's a clear framework for using creatine strategically:

**If your primary goal is muscle performance:** 3–5g daily remains effective and well-supported by decades of research.

**If your primary goal is cognitive performance and brain health:** Aim for 10g daily, split into two 5g doses taken with meals for best absorption and to minimize any GI discomfort.

**On high-demand days — sleep-deprived, jet-lagged, facing heavy cognitive load:** Consider 20g for that day, split into two 10g doses. This is exactly what both Dr. Patrick and Dr. Candow do themselves.

**If you're vegetarian or vegan:** The cognitive case for higher dosing is especially strong. Dietary creatine comes almost exclusively from meat and fish — plant-based eaters have significantly lower baseline creatine stores in both muscle and brain, making supplementation more impactful and higher doses more justified.

**Timing:** Unlike caffeine, creatine is not acute. It takes several weeks to build up in your tissues and have a noticeable effect — it's not something you take when feeling terrible for an immediate boost. [Life Extension](https://www.lifeextension.com/wellness/supplements/biohacking-supplements) Timing within the day matters less than consistency. Take it whenever you'll remember it.

## Safety at Higher Doses

A common concern when increasing from 5g to 10g is safety. The research is reassuring. Creatine has a strong safety profile, supported by consistent tolerability across studies with doses ranging from 3g to 10g per day. [Thrivetools](https://thrivetools.co/product-category/organic-foods/) The primary side effect reported at higher doses is gastrointestinal discomfort — which is almost entirely resolved by splitting the dose across two meals rather than taking it all at once.

The old concern about kidney damage has been thoroughly debunked in healthy individuals across multiple long-term studies. Creatine raises serum creatinine (a kidney marker) as a metabolic byproduct — but this is a false positive, not an indicator of kidney stress. Anyone with pre-existing kidney conditions should consult their physician before increasing dose.
